How Was Your Team in February?
February was a month of two clear halves. The first two weeks were the hardest — high professional flag rates, low Physical and Recovery scores, staff arriving already under pressure. By Week 3, all five dimensions had recovered significantly. The team ended the month stronger than they started it.

The most important signal: work conditions, not personal life, drove almost all low scores — and those conditions improved as the month went on. This is directly within your control as a manager.

Every single dimension improved week-on-week. Week 3 was the strongest week of the month across all five dimensions. Understanding what changed between Week 1 and Week 3 is the most important question going into March.

Work-Related Flags by Week

71 professional flags in Week 1, just 9 in Week 3. This is not a personal wellbeing story — it is a working conditions story. When conditions improved, flags almost disappeared.

Note on post-shift data: Only 34 of 167 check-ins (20%) were post-shift. Reliable post-shift data only exists for Feb 12 (8 check-ins) and Feb 13 (10 check-ins). After that, most days had only 1 post-shift response — too few to draw conclusions from. Increasing post-shift participation is the single biggest data gap for March.

This Month's Focus — March 2026
Three actions derived directly from the data and from what your team said. Not programmes, not initiatives — specific things you can do this week that will move the numbers that matter most.
1
Urgent · Kitchen · This Week
Build a proper end to the kitchen shift
BOH Physical (2.87) and Recovery (2.81) are the only scores in this report below the watch threshold. Week 1 post-shift data — the only post-shift sample large enough to be valid — showed Physical dropping sharply by end of service. Kitchen staff are leaving exhausted and not arriving recovered. This is the single most urgent number in the report.
Do this: At the end of each kitchen shift, build in 5–10 minutes before staff leave — a fixed, expected moment. A brief stretch, a quiet sit-down, a slower transition out of service pace. Consistent and deliberate. The signal it sends — that the working day has a proper end — matters as much as the time itself. Start this week.
2
Structural · This Month
Understand what made Week 3 different from Week 1
Professional flags dropped from 71 in Week 1 to 9 in Week 3. That is not random — something about working conditions changed. The Feb 24 Monday had the highest average score of the entire month (3.92) with only 2 flags. Feb 27 had zero flags for the first time. Something specific happened in that last week that the first week did not have. Your team felt it.
Do this: Think back deliberately. What was different in Week 3? Service flow? Staffing levels? How briefings were run? Whether the kitchen was set up before service? Small structural differences produce outsized effects on how staff feel. If you can identify what changed and protect it in March, scores will continue improving.
3
Programme · Ongoing
Get more post-shift check-ins
Only 20% of check-ins in February were post-shift (34 of 167). The days with the most post-shift data — Feb 12 and Feb 13 — are the only days where we can actually see what a shift cost each person. Without more post-shift data, the March report will still be missing half the picture. This is the most important data gap to close.
Do this: One verbal mention at end of service, every night: “Don’t forget your 60-second check-in before you go.” Nothing more required. Emphasise it is anonymous. Target for March: above 30% post-shift participation. That would give enough data to compare pre and post meaningfully for the first time.
